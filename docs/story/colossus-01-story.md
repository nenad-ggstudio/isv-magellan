# Colossus-01

## Captain Harrison

"Well, this bucket is going to be my home for a very long time."

Captain William Harrison stood beneath the vast transparent dome of the Orbital Planetary Shipyard and stared at the ship that would carry ten thousand souls across the darkness between stars.

Colossus-01 hung motionless beyond the glass.

Even after months of briefings, simulations, inspections, and political ceremonies, the sight of it still felt unreal.

The vessel stretched nearly six hundred meters from bow to stern, a massive cylindrical structure surrounded by docking arms, construction platforms, and swarms of maintenance craft that looked like insects beside it. Floodlights from the shipyard illuminated its silver-gray hull, making it appear less like a spacecraft and more like a small city torn from the surface of a planet and suspended in space.

Humanity had built larger structures before.

Orbital habitats.

Solar collectors.

The Lunar elevator.

But those structures stayed put.

Colossus-01 was expected to travel twenty-five light years into the unknown.

Harrison folded his arms behind his back.

During the war he had commanded armies numbering in the millions. He had watched continents burn. He had signed orders that sent men and women to their deaths by the tens of thousands.

Yet somehow this frightened him more. Perhaps because this time there would be no reinforcements. Once the ship departed, everyone aboard would belong to history.

"Beautiful, isn't she?"

The voice came from behind him.

Harrison turned.

Commander Ying Zhao approached with measured steps, hands clasped behind her back.

She looked young to him, though she was nearly forty. Tall, composed, and always carrying herself with the confidence of someone who genuinely believed the future could be better than the past.

The perfect officer for the Earth Union.

The perfect political choice for his executive officer.

"That's one word for it," Harrison said.

Ying smiled.

"You don't agree?"

"I've seen enough military projects to know that anything this large usually comes with twice as many problems as engineers admit."

"The engineers would probably agree with you."

That earned the first hint of a smile from Harrison.

They stood together for a moment, watching maintenance craft drift around the colossal hull.

Far below them, Earth filled half the sky. It always looked peaceful from orbit.

Hard to believe four billion people had died fighting over that world.

The Earth Union had been born from those ashes. Politicians called it humanity's greatest achievement. Schools taught children that the old rivalries had finally ended.

Harrison wasn't so certain.

People had changed their flags and traded one uniform for another. But whether they had truly changed themselves, he had always been skeptical. Perhaps that skepticism lingered because, deep down, he had never believed he had changed either.

He glanced toward Ying.

She represented everything the Union wanted to become. A citizen of a united world rather than a particular nation. An officer selected for merit, not geography. A believer.

Harrison trusted her competence. But trusting the ideals she represented was another matter entirely. In the end, she was an Easterner, and that fact was not so easily set aside.

"You know what I find funny?" he asked.

Ying raised an eyebrow.

"What's that, Captain?"

"We spent centuries fighting over one planet."

He pointed toward Earth.

"Now we're building a ship to carry our arguments somewhere else."

For a moment Ying said nothing.

Then she looked back toward Colossus-01.

"Maybe."

Her reflection appeared faintly against the glass.

"Or maybe we're carrying the lessons."

Harrison grunted.

"Let's hope you're right, Commander."

Neither of them noticed the worker standing on a maintenance platform several hundred meters away.

The man wasn't looking at the ship.

He wasn't looking at Earth.

His attention was fixed entirely on them.
